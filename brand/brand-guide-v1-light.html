Brand identity · v1.0 · working draft

Skin over Steel

A community for men's sexual health that puts the human before the machine — evidence-graded education, biohacker curiosity, and pseudonymous peer support. The name is the thesis: what's warm and alive outranks what's rigid and armored. The abbreviation is the promise: SOS — a signal, answered.

01

Foundation

Everything in the identity is derived from two ideas already inside the name.

Skin / Steel duality

Every surface pairs a warm, organic voice (serif, italic, copper) with a rigid, engineered structure (condensed grotesque, gunmetal). The warm element always sits on top or in front — skin over steel, never the reverse. This is the layout rule, the logo rule, and the editorial rule: humanity leads, hardware supports.

SOS · the answered signal

The initials happen to be the world's oldest distress call. We claim it deliberately: asking for help here is normal, expected, and answered. The Morse pattern ··· ––– ··· is the brand's signature device — used as a divider, a loading state, and a discreet mark members recognize.

Personality in five words

Candid · Evidence-led · Unashamed · Curious · Discreet. The brand talks like a well-read friend who trains, reads the studies, and doesn't flinch — never like a pharma ad, a locker room, or a supplement funnel.

02

Logo system

Three coordinated marks. The wordmark leads on editorial surfaces; the monogram works at avatar and favicon sizes; the signal mark is the members-only shorthand.

A · Primary wordmark. Italic serif "Skin" in copper physically overlaps the gunmetal "STEEL" block — the name performed, not just set.

B · Monogram. A warm circle (skin) laid over a steel hexagon (structure). Scales to favicon; the hexagon nods to lab/engineering without turning clinical.

C · Signal mark. Morse SOS with copper dashes. Divider between article sections, loading animation, sticker, and profile flair. Deliberately illegible to outsiders — discretion is a feature.

Rules: never place STEEL over Skin · minimum clear space = height of one dash · monogram never carries a tagline · signal mark never recolored outside palette.

05

Evidence grading — a brand component

Trust is the product. Every claim on the site carries one of three visible grades, styled as brand objects so readers learn the system at a glance. This is also what separates SOS from supplement marketing in the eyes of readers, search engines, and AI agents.

Established

Consistent clinical evidence, guideline-level. Example: cardiovascular exercise, sleep, and pelvic floor therapy as first-line factors.

Emerging

Early or mixed research; promising but unproven. Clearly framed as "what the studies show so far," never as a protocol to copy.

Community anecdote

One member's experience, n = 1. Valuable for morale and questions to bring to a clinician — visually fenced off from medical content.

06

Voice & tone

Write like the friend who read the study. Direct about anatomy, generous with context, allergic to hype and shame alike.

Not this

"Struggling in the bedroom? Reclaim your masculinity with this one ancient trick doctors won't tell you."

This

"ED has a cause, and causes can be found. Here's the workup a good clinician runs — and what you can start measuring tonight."

Not this

"Erectile dysfunction affects patients' quality-of-life outcomes across multiple psychosocial domains."

This

"This affects how you feel about yourself, your relationship, and your future. That's worth taking seriously — so let's."

Not this

"Stack these 5 compounds for guaranteed results 🚀"

This

"Here's what the evidence grades say — and why 'guaranteed' is the word that should make you close the tab."

Vocabulary

  • Say: erectile function, members, evidence grade, signal, workup, protocol (for measurable habits only)
  • Avoid: "performance," "manhood," "cure," "hack" as a noun, any euphemism that implies shame
  • Members are: members. No cute collective noun. Discretion beats branding here.

Hard lines

  • Never dosing instructions for prescription or gray-market compounds — in editorial or UGC
  • Always "talk to a clinician" framed as the smart move, not a legal disclaimer mumble
  • Always a visible path to mental-health resources; low mood and ED travel together
